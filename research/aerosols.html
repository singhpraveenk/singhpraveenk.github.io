Atmospheric Aerosols

Understanding the Black Carbon dynamics and Black Carbon-Cryosphere Interactions in the Hindu Kush Himalayan Region

The Himalayas are often called the “Third Pole” because they hold more ice than anywhere else on Earth outside the Arctic and Antarctic. Nearly two billion people depend on the rivers that flow from these mountains. My research focuses on a simple but powerful question:

How is air pollution—especially black carbon—changing the future of Himalayan snow and glaciers?

To answer this, I work across the Hindu Kush Himalayan (HKH) region, and my research focuses on understanding how black carbon (BC) and other light‑absorbing aerosols influence the Hindu Kush Himalayan (HKH) region, one of the world’s most climate‑sensitive mountain systems. Through a combination of high‑altitude fieldwork, ground-based observations, chemical transport modeling, remote sensing, and radiative transfer simulations, my work quantifies how aerosols are transported, how they modify atmospheric and surface energy budgets, and how they accelerate snow and glacier melt.


What is black carbon, and why does it matter?

Black carbon (BC) is a tiny particle released when we burn things—diesel, firewood, crop residue, forest biomass. It’s dark, so it absorbs sunlight extremely well. When BC lands on snow or ice, it darkens the surface, accelerating melting.

Think of it like wearing a black T‑shirt on a sunny day—you feel warmer. Glaciers feel the same effect.


Black Carbon in the Central Himalaya: Concentrations, Sources, and Cryospheric Impacts

High-altitude BC observations near Yala Glacier

At 4900 m a.s.l., near the Yala Glacier, one of the highest BC monitoring efforts in the HKH. Year-long measurements revealed:

  • Peak atmospheric BC concentrations during pre-monsoon (up to 0.73 μg m⁻³)
  • BC in surface snow ranging from 96–542 ng g⁻¹, sufficient to measurably darken the glacier surface
  • Surface albedo reduction of 0.8–3.8%, simulated using the SNICAR model
  • BC-induced glacier mass loss contributes ~39% of the total melt during the pre-monsoon
These findings highlight the disproportionate sensitivity of Himalayan glaciers to even small amounts of deposited BC.

Source attribution

Using both in situ absorption Ångström exponent (AAE) analysis and WRF-Chem tagged tracer simulations, we quantified BC origins:

  • ~44% from biomass burning (in situ AAE)
  • ~77% from anthropogenic sources (model-based)
  • Major regional contributors: Nepal + India, followed by China
This dual-method approach provided one of the most robust BC source assessments for a Himalayan glacier environment.


Regional BC Variability Across the Hindu Kush Himalaya

To address the severe observational gap in the central Himalaya, nine long-term BC monitoring stations were establihsed across Nepal, Bhutan, and the Tibetan Plateau (102–4920 m a.s.l.). Analysis of 2016–2019 data revealed:

Extreme spatial heterogeneity

  • Annual mean BC ranged from 36 ng m⁻³ (high-altitude) to 45,737 ng m⁻³ (urban/lowland)
  • High-altitude sites peaked in pre-monsoon, while low-altitude sites peaked in winter

Dominance of biomass burning

Across most sites, biomass burning contributed >50% of BC, except in Kathmandu, where fossil fuel sources were more prominent.

Long-range transport pathways

Using concentration-weighted trajectories (CWT) and frequency analysis, we found:

  • Wintertime pollution is strongly influenced by westerly long-range transport
  • Monsoon brings South Asian emissions deep into the mountains
  • Valley dynamics amplify pollution in mid-altitude basins
This work provides the most comprehensive BC climatology for the HKH region to date.


Aerosol Composition, Radiative Forcing, and Atmospheric Heating in the Central Himalaya

Using AERONET, CALIPSO, and radiative transfer modeling, I quantified the contributions of BC, organic carbon (OC/BrC), and dust to aerosol absorption and atmospheric heating across nine stations (110–4901 m a.s.l.).

Key findings

  • BC dominates absorption (>50%) at most mid- and high-altitude sites
  • Dust influence increases toward low-altitude Indo-Gangetic Plain sites
  • CALIPSO profiles show significant aerosol loading above 3 km, with carbonaceous aerosols present even above 5 km
  • Radiative forcing increases toward lower altitudes but remains substantial at high elevations
  • Vertical heating rates reveal strong atmospheric warming, critical for glacier mass balance and monsoon dynamics
This work improves the representation of aerosol–radiation interactions in climate models and highlights the vulnerability of Himalayan glaciers to elevated aerosol layers.


Overall Contribution

Across these studies, my research provides:

  • High-elevation BC observations in one of the world’s most remote cryospheric environments
  • Quantitative attribution of BC sources using both observational and modeling approaches
  • Evidence of BC-driven glacier melt, including one of the first estimates of its fractional contribution (~39%)
  • A regional-scale assessment of BC variability across the HKH
  • Aerosol composition and radiative forcing estimates essential for climate modeling
Together, these works advance our understanding of how aerosols shape the climate and cryosphere of the Himalaya—informing mitigation strategies, regional policy, and future climate projections.


Why this research matters

The Himalaya is warming faster than the global average. Understanding how pollution accelerates glacier melt is essential for:

  • Water security for millions of people
  • Disaster risk reduction (floods, landslides, glacial lake outbursts)
  • Climate policy in South Asia
  • Protecting fragile mountain ecosystems
My work helps identify which pollutants, from which regions, and during which seasons have the biggest impact—so that governments and communities can take targeted action.


Priority Policy Actions

  1. Accelerate clean household energy transitions

    2. Replace biomass cookstoves with clean alternatives in rural and mountain communities.

  2. Regulate and modernize brick kilns and small industries

    3. Adopt cleaner kiln technologies and enforce emissions standards.

  3. Reduce agricultural residue burning

    4. Promote mechanized alternatives, incentives, and community‑level enforcement.

  4. Strengthen forest fire management

    5. Invest in early detection, rapid response, and community fire‑prevention programs.

  5. Expand high‑altitude monitoring networks

    6. Support long‑term BC and aerosol monitoring to guide policy and track progress.

  6. Build a regional BC mitigation framework

    7. Facilitate cooperation among Nepal, India, Bhutan, China, and Pakistan on emissions reduction and data sharing.
